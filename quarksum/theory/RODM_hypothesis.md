**Author:** Aaron Rhodes
**Date:** March 2026
**Status:** Pre-print / Working Paper

## Abstract

We propose a cosmogonic model in which the observable universe nucleated inside a
black hole of a parent universe via a first-order phase transition. Parent-universe
matter accumulates in the black hole interior over prolonged timescales, forming
a radial density gradient densest at the core. When the central density crosses a
critical threshold, a phase transition ignites and propagates outward as a
Chapman--Jouguet detonation wave. Only the innermost core — a fraction
\(\xi \approx 0.158\) of the total matter — converts to locally compatible
baryonic matter. The vast majority, including all matter beyond the conversion
radius and all subsequently accreted material, persists as *remnant matter*:
gravitationally active but inert under Standard Model gauge interactions. We
identify this remnant with dark matter. The detonation homogenizes the
post-transition matter distribution, solving the horizon problem without
inflationary expansion. Geometric flatness (\(\Omega = 1\)) follows from
the detonation shockwave expanding uniformly from the conversion center. The density dependence of
conversion efficiency seeds primordial perturbations whose spectral shape is
determined by the pre-transition density profile rather than quantum fluctuations
of an inflaton. We derive the model from an effective action, obtain the
conversion efficiency from Landau--Ginzburg thermodynamics constrained by Planck
data, formalize the radial criticality and conversion radius, construct the
Boltzmann hierarchy for remnant perturbations, and extract six falsifiable
predictions — including a lensing non-Gaussianity excess, a null
direct-detection signal, and anomalous primordial spectral running — testable
with Euclid, Rubin/LSST, and next-generation xenon detectors within the coming
decade. We additionally outline a speculative extension in which residual
incompatible binding energy from the conversion event manifests as dark energy.

## 1. Introduction and Motivation

### 1.1 The Dark Matter Problem

Multiple independent lines of evidence establish that approximately 84% of the
matter content of the universe is non-luminous:

- **Galaxy rotation curves.** Flat rotation profiles at large radii imply mass
  distributions extending well beyond the visible disk (Rubin & Ford 1970).

- **Gravitational lensing.** Weak-lensing mass reconstructions of galaxy
  clusters, most dramatically the Bullet Cluster (1E 0657-56), reveal an 8σ
  spatial offset between the center of total mass and the center of baryonic
  mass, demonstrating the existence of a dissipationless matter component that
  cannot be explained by modified gravity (Clowe et al. 2006).

- **CMB acoustic peaks.** The relative heights of the acoustic peaks in the
  cosmic microwave background power spectrum require a cold, pressureless matter
  component with density \(\Omega_c h^2 = 0.1193 \pm 0.0009\), roughly five
  times the baryon density \(\Omega_b h^2 = 0.0224 \pm 0.0001\) (Planck
  Collaboration 2020a).

- **Large-scale structure.** The distribution and clustering of galaxies on
  scales of tens to hundreds of megaparsecs is consistent with gravitational
  amplification of primordial perturbations in a cold dark matter (CDM)
  dominated universe (Springel et al. 2005).

### 1.2 The Origin Problem

The standard \(\Lambda\)CDM cosmological model provides an excellent
phenomenological framework for describing dark matter's gravitational effects
but remains agnostic about its fundamental origin. Proposed candidates — weakly
interacting massive particles (WIMPs), axions, sterile neutrinos — are motivated
by extensions to the Standard Model but have so far eluded direct detection.
The LUX-ZEPLIN (LZ) experiment excludes spin-independent WIMP-nucleon cross
sections above \(9.2 \times 10^{-48}\ \text{cm}^2\) at 36 GeV/c² (LZ
Collaboration 2023), and XENONnT sets comparable limits at
\(2.58 \times 10^{-47}\ \text{cm}^2\) at 28 GeV/c² (XENONnT Collaboration
2023). These null results progressively constrain the available parameter space
for any dark matter candidate that couples to Standard Model fields.

This paper proposes a different explanation: dark matter is not a new particle
species produced by SM-adjacent physics, but rather a *cosmogonic remnant* —
matter inherited from a parent universe that never converted to locally
compatible form during the phase transition that initiated our Big Bang.

### 1.3 Prior Related Work

The hypothesis builds on three existing theoretical threads:

1. **Cosmological Natural Selection (Smolin 1992).** Smolin proposed that black
   hole singularities "bounce" into new universes with slightly modified
   physical constants, creating a selection mechanism favoring universes that
   produce many black holes. RODM adopts the parent-child universe topology
   while providing a specific mechanism for the matter content of the daughter
   universe.

2. **Einstein--Cartan Torsion Cosmology (Popławski 2010).** Popławski showed
   that incorporating spacetime torsion (from fermion spin) into general
   relativity prevents singularity formation inside black holes. Matter
   rebounds at extreme density (~\(10^{50}\) kg/m³) in his framework,
   producing a "Big Bounce." RODM notes this density scale as a physical
   reference point but does not adopt the bounce mechanism. In RODM, flatness
   and homogeneity arise from the Chapman--Jouguet detonation shockwave, not
   from torsion-mediated rebound. The mechanisms are distinct.

3. **Mirror Matter / Shadow Sectors (Foot, Lew & Volkas 1991).** Mirror-matter
   models postulate a hidden sector of particles that interact gravitationally
   with ordinary matter but not through SM gauge forces. RODM arrives at a
   phenomenologically similar dark sector but from a cosmogonic origin rather
   than a symmetry principle.

### 1.4 Scope of This Paper

We present the Remnant-Origin Dark Matter (RODM) model as a falsifiable
hypothesis. Section 2 gives the qualitative model, including the
accumulation, radial criticality, and shockwave homogenization mechanisms.
Section 3 develops the full mathematical framework: effective action,
phase-transition thermodynamics, radial density profiles, detonation
dynamics, perturbation theory, and observational predictions. Section 4
summarizes the quantitative predictions. Section 5 maps them onto current
and planned experiments. Section 6 discusses the relationship to prior work,
presents a speculative dark energy extension, and addresses limitations.
Section 7 concludes.

## 2. The RODM Model

### 2.1 Premise

The observable universe occupies the interior of a black hole that formed in a
parent universe. The parent universe possesses its own physical laws, matter
content, and spacetime structure. Matter from the parent universe falls through
the event horizon and accumulates in the interior.

### 2.2 Accumulation and Radial Criticality

The black hole interior does not convert its contents all at once. Matter
from the parent universe falls through the event horizon and accumulates over
a prolonged period — potentially billions of years in parent-universe time.
An accretion disk forms on the exterior, feeding a growing reservoir of
parent-universe matter within.

The infalling matter fills the inescapable gravity well, with density
increasing toward the center where gravitational compression is greatest.
The result is a radial density profile: densest at the core, progressively
sparser toward the periphery. As matter continues to arrive, the energetic
pressure of the interior rises, but the material remains distributed
non-uniformly — always densest at the center, thinning outward.

### 2.3 The Conversion Event

At the core, where density is highest, the matter eventually crosses a
critical density threshold — on the order of \(\sim 10^{50}\) kg/m³,
consistent with independent estimates from torsion cosmology (Popławski 2010).
A first-order phase
transition ignites *at the center* and propagates outward as a detonation
wave. During the transition, parent-universe matter converts to baryonic
matter compatible with the local spacetime's gauge structure.

Crucially, the conversion does not process all matter. The detonation wave
weakens as it propagates through progressively sparser material. At some
radius \(r_{\text{conv}}\), the shock energy drops below the conversion
threshold. Matter beyond this radius — the vast majority by volume — remains
unconverted. This is the *remnant matter* (dark matter).

Furthermore, the parent universe's black hole continues to accrete. Matter
that falls through the event horizon *after* the conversion event enters a
universe that has already undergone its Big Bang. This late-arriving matter
was never subjected to the phase transition and contributes directly to the
dark matter inventory. The conversion is thus a one-time event acting on a
fraction of the total matter content, while accretion is ongoing.

The conversion fraction is not a free parameter. It is fixed by the
thermodynamics of the phase transition — specifically, the radial density
profile at the moment of ignition and the energy budget of the detonation —
and must reproduce the observed ratio:

\[
\xi = \frac{\Omega_b}{\Omega_b + \Omega_c}
= \frac{0.0224}{0.0224 + 0.1193} \approx 0.158
\]

The smallness of \(\xi\) is naturally explained: only the innermost,
densest core participates in the conversion. The overwhelming majority of
the matter — both the material beyond \(r_{\text{conv}}\) at the time of
conversion and all subsequently accreted material — remains as dark matter.

### 2.4 Properties of Remnant Matter

Remnant matter retains the following properties:

| Property | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| **Gravitationally active** | Couples to the metric tensor \(g_{\mu\nu}\) like all matter |
| **SM-gauge-inert** | Parent-universe gauge charges are incompatible with local SU(3)×SU(2)×U(1) |
| **Collisionless** | No self-interaction via local forces; consistent with Bullet Cluster σ/m < 1 cm²/g (Markevitch et al. 2004) |
| **Cold** | Kinetic energy thermalized during black hole accumulation and the conversion event |
| **Stable** | No accessible decay channels into local-universe particles |

### 2.5 Shockwave Homogenization

The first-order phase transition releases latent heat. Because matter density is
highest at the center of the black hole interior (where accumulation was
greatest), the energy release is strongest there. The resulting detonation wave
propagates outward, homogenizing the matter distribution:

- Near the center: maximum conversion, maximum energy release, strongest shock
- At intermediate radii: moderate conversion, diminishing shock strength
- At the periphery: minimal conversion, shock has dissipated

This density-dependent detonation naturally produces a nearly homogeneous
post-transition matter distribution, solving the horizon problem without
requiring a separate inflationary epoch. Geometric flatness (\(\Omega = 1\))
follows from energy conservation in the detonation: the shockwave deposits
equal energy per unit volume across the causal domain, driving the total
energy density to the critical value \(\rho_c = 3H^2/8\pi G\). No
torsion-bounce dynamics are required; the flatness is a consequence of the
detonation geometry itself.

### 2.6 Origin of Structure

The conversion efficiency \(\xi\) depends on the local matter density at the
time of the phase transition. Regions of slightly higher initial density
convert slightly more matter, producing small baryon-density perturbations.
These perturbations seed the primordial density fluctuations that grow under
gravitational instability to form galaxies and large-scale structure. The
shape of the resulting power spectrum depends on the pre-transition density
profile of the black hole interior, providing a distinguishable alternative
to the nearly scale-invariant spectrum predicted by single-field inflation.

## 3. Mathematical Framework

### 3.1 Remnant Sector Effective Action

We model the remnant as a real scalar field \(\phi_R\) minimally coupled to
gravity with no Standard Model gauge couplings. The total action is:

\[
S = S_{\text{EH}} + S_{\text{SM}} + S_R
\]

where \(S_{\text{EH}}\) is the Einstein--Hilbert gravitational action,
\(S_{\text{SM}}\) is the Standard Model action, and the remnant sector is:

\[
S_R = \int d^4x \, \sqrt{-g} \left[
\frac{1}{2} g^{\mu\nu} \partial_\mu \phi_R \, \partial_\nu \phi_R
- V(\phi_R)
\right]
\]

For the potential, we adopt the simplest form consistent with collisionless,
non-self-interacting dark matter:

\[
V(\phi_R) = \frac{1}{2} m_R^2 \, \phi_R^2
\]

This free-field potential produces a pressureless dust in the non-relativistic
limit, consistent with the collisionless behavior observed in cluster mergers
(Markevitch et al. 2004). The *only* coupling between \(S_R\) and
\(S_{\text{SM}}\) is through the shared metric \(g_{\mu\nu}\) — the remnant
interacts purely gravitationally.

**Stress-energy tensor** of the remnant field:

\[
T_{\mu\nu}^{(R)} = \partial_\mu \phi_R \, \partial_\nu \phi_R
- g_{\mu\nu} \left[
\frac{1}{2} g^{\alpha\beta} \partial_\alpha \phi_R \, \partial_\beta \phi_R
- V(\phi_R)
\right]
\]

In the non-relativistic, homogeneous limit, this reduces to a perfect fluid
with energy density \(\rho_R = \frac{1}{2}\dot{\phi}_R^2 + V(\phi_R)\) and
pressure \(p_R = \frac{1}{2}\dot{\phi}_R^2 - V(\phi_R)\). For a
non-relativistic condensate oscillating in the quadratic potential,
\(\langle p_R \rangle \approx 0\), recovering standard cold dark matter
phenomenology at late times.

### 3.2 Phase Transition Thermodynamics

We model the Big Bang conversion event as a first-order phase transition using
Landau--Ginzburg theory. Define a scalar order parameter \(\eta\) representing
the degree of matter conversion:

- \(\eta = 0\): unconverted parent-universe matter (remnant / dark matter)
- \(\eta = 1\): fully converted baryonic matter

The Gibbs free energy density as a function of \(\eta\) and temperature \(T\):

\[
f(\eta, T) = f_0 + \frac{1}{2} a(T)\, \eta^2
- \frac{1}{3} c\, \eta^3
+ \frac{1}{4} b\, \eta^4
\]

where \(a(T) = a_0(T - T_c)\), with \(T_c\) the critical temperature of the
conversion transition, and \(b > 0\) ensures stability. The cubic term
(\(c > 0\)) makes the transition first-order: the order parameter jumps
discontinuously from \(\eta = 0\) to a nonzero equilibrium value \(\eta_{\text{eq}}\)
at the transition temperature, releasing latent heat.

**Equilibrium condition.** Minimizing \(f\) with respect to \(\eta\):

\[
\frac{\partial f}{\partial \eta} = a(T)\,\eta - c\,\eta^2 + b\,\eta^3 = 0
\]

The nontrivial solution (setting \(\eta \neq 0\), dividing through) is:

\[
\eta_{\text{eq}} = \frac{c \pm \sqrt{c^2 - 4\,a(T)\,b}}{2b}
\]

At the transition temperature \(T = T^*\) where the converted and unconverted
phases have equal free energy (\(f(\eta_{\text{eq}}, T^*) = f(0, T^*)\)), the
equilibrium value is:

\[
\eta_{\text{eq}}(T^*) = \frac{2c}{3b}
\]

**Latent heat.** At the coexistence temperature \(T^*\), the two phases have
equal free energy by definition: \(f(0, T^*) = f(\eta_{\text{eq}}, T^*)\).
The latent heat is therefore not the free energy difference (which vanishes)
but the entropy discontinuity between the two phases:

\[
L = T^* \Delta S = T^* \left(-\frac{\partial f}{\partial T}\bigg|_{\eta_{\text{eq}}}
+ \frac{\partial f}{\partial T}\bigg|_{\eta=0}\right)
\]

Since \(a(T) = a_0(T - T_c)\) and the \(\eta = 0\) phase has no
\(T\)-dependent contribution:

\[
L = \frac{1}{2}\, a_0\, T^*\, \eta_{\text{eq}}^2
= \frac{1}{2}\, a_0\, T^* \left(\frac{2c}{3b}\right)^2
= \frac{2\, a_0\, c^2\, T^*}{9\, b^2}
\]

**Constraining with Planck data.** The conversion fraction — the fraction of
total matter that transitions to the baryonic phase — is identified with the
cosmological baryon fraction:

\[
\xi \equiv \frac{\Omega_b}{\Omega_b + \Omega_c}
= \frac{0.02242}{0.02242 + 0.11933} = 0.1582 \pm 0.0010
\]

using Planck 2018 values (Planck Collaboration 2020a). This value is stable
across independent CMB datasets: Planck 2020 (PR4) gives \(\xi = 0.1571\) and
ACT DR6 (2024) gives \(\xi = 0.1611\), a spread of \(\sim 2.5\%\). The model's
structural claim — that the baryon fraction of total matter sets the conversion
efficiency — does not depend on \(\xi\) taking any particular value. This
constrains the Landau coefficients: in a volume-weighted average over the
transition region, the mean order parameter must satisfy
\(\langle \eta \rangle = \xi\).

For a first-order transition occurring in a fraction \(f_V\) of the total
volume with strength \(\eta_{\text{eq}}\):

\[
\xi = f_V \cdot \eta_{\text{eq}}
\]

If the entire volume transitions (\(f_V = 1\)), then
\(\eta_{\text{eq}} = \xi \approx 0.158\), and the Landau coefficients satisfy:

\[
\frac{2c}{3b} = 0.158 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad c = 0.237\, b
\]

This is the primary constraint linking the microphysics of the conversion
(encoded in the Landau coefficients) to cosmological observables.

### 3.3 Radial Criticality, Detonation, and Homogenization

The phase transition does not occur uniformly. The pre-transition density
profile inside the black hole determines where conversion ignites, how far
the detonation propagates, and what fraction of matter converts.

#### 3.3.1 Pre-Transition Density Profile

Matter accreted from the parent universe accumulates under gravity, producing
a radial density profile. For a quasi-static, self-gravitating accumulation
(analogous to a singular isothermal sphere), the density scales as:

\[
\rho(r) = \rho_c \left(\frac{r_c}{r}\right)^{\alpha_{\text{BH}}}
\]

where \(\rho_c\) is the central density, \(r_c\) is a core scale radius, and
\(\alpha_{\text{BH}}\) is the density power-law index. For an isothermal
sphere, \(\alpha_{\text{BH}} = 2\); for a more concentrated profile,
\(\alpha_{\text{BH}} > 2\).

#### 3.3.2 Conversion Radius

The phase transition ignites where the density first exceeds the critical
threshold \(\rho_{\text{crit}} \sim 10^{50}\) kg/m³. The detonation
propagates outward, but the shock weakens as it traverses progressively
sparser material. At the conversion radius \(r_{\text{conv}}\), the shock
energy drops below the threshold needed to drive the transition:

\[
r_{\text{conv}} = r_c \left(\frac{\rho_c}{\rho_{\text{crit}}}\right)^{1/\alpha_{\text{BH}}}
\]

All matter within \(r < r_{\text{conv}}\) undergoes conversion; matter beyond
this radius remains as remnant (dark matter). The conversion fraction is then:

\[
\xi = \frac{M(r < r_{\text{conv}})}{M_{\text{total}}}
= \frac{\int_0^{r_{\text{conv}}} 4\pi r^2 \rho(r)\, dr}
       {\int_0^{R_{\text{BH}}} 4\pi r^2 \rho(r)\, dr}
\]

For \(\alpha_{\text{BH}} < 3\) (so the mass integral converges), this
evaluates to:

\[
\xi = \left(\frac{r_{\text{conv}}}{R_{\text{BH}}}\right)^{3-\alpha_{\text{BH}}}
\]

With \(\xi = 0.158\) and \(\alpha_{\text{BH}} \approx 2\):

\[
\frac{r_{\text{conv}}}{R_{\text{BH}}} = \xi^{1/(3-\alpha_{\text{BH}})}
= 0.158^{1/1} = 0.158
\]

The converted core occupies only ~16% of the total radius, or ~0.4% of the
total volume — a small, dense kernel surrounded by a vast reservoir of
unconverted remnant matter. This geometrically explains the 5.36:1
dark-matter-to-baryon ratio.

#### 3.3.3 Ongoing Accretion

The parent-universe black hole continues to accrete matter after the
conversion event. This late-arriving matter enters a universe that has
already undergone its phase transition. It was never subjected to the
detonation and contributes directly to the remnant (dark matter) inventory.

The observed \(\xi\) therefore represents a *lower bound* on the conversion
efficiency of the initial event, since subsequent accretion dilutes the
baryon fraction over time. If accretion has contributed a fraction
\(f_{\text{acc}}\) of the current dark matter inventory, the intrinsic
conversion efficiency of the phase transition was:

\[
\xi_{\text{intrinsic}} = \frac{\xi}{1 - f_{\text{acc}}(1-\xi)}
\]

For modest ongoing accretion (\(f_{\text{acc}} \ll 1\)),
\(\xi_{\text{intrinsic}} \approx \xi\) and the correction is negligible.

#### 3.3.4 Chapman--Jouguet Detonation

Following standard detonation theory (Chapman 1899; Jouguet 1905), the
detonation front propagates at the Chapman--Jouguet velocity:

\[
v_{\text{CJ}} = \sqrt{\frac{2(\gamma^2 - 1)\, L}{\rho_0}}
\]

where \(\gamma\) is the effective adiabatic index of the post-transition
material and \(\rho_0\) is the pre-transition density. The post-shock density
jump across the detonation front satisfies the Rankine--Hugoniot condition:

\[
\frac{\rho_1}{\rho_0} = \frac{(\gamma + 1)}{(\gamma - 1)}
\]

For a relativistic fluid (\(\gamma = 4/3\)), this gives a compression ratio
of 7. For a non-relativistic ideal gas (\(\gamma = 5/3\)), the ratio is 4.

#### 3.3.5 Sedov--Taylor Expansion

After the initial detonation, the blast wave enters the Sedov--Taylor
self-similar expansion phase (Sedov 1959; Taylor 1950). The shock radius
evolves as:

\[
R_s(t) = \left(\frac{E_0}{\alpha_s\, \rho_0}\right)^{1/5} t^{2/5}
\]

where \(E_0\) is the total energy released and \(\alpha_s\) is a dimensionless
constant of order unity depending on \(\gamma\). The shock velocity:

\[
v_s(t) = \frac{2}{5} \frac{R_s}{t} \propto t^{-3/5}
\]

The energy flux (shock strength) at radius \(r\) scales as:

\[
S(r) \propto \frac{E_0}{4\pi r^2}
\]

This radial dependence is central to the homogenization argument: the shock
is strongest where initial density and conversion are highest (the core) and
weakens toward the periphery where less matter remains.

#### 3.3.6 Homogenization Condition

For the shockwave to effectively homogenize the matter distribution, it must
traverse the causal volume before significant gravitational collapse can begin.
This requires:

\[
t_{\text{shock}} \ll t_{\text{ff}}
\]

where the shock crossing time is:

\[
t_{\text{shock}} \sim \frac{R}{v_{\text{CJ}}}
\]

and the gravitational free-fall time is:

\[
t_{\text{ff}} \sim \frac{1}{\sqrt{G\,\rho_0}}
\]

The condition becomes:

\[
\frac{R}{v_{\text{CJ}}} \ll \frac{1}{\sqrt{G\,\rho_0}}
\]

Substituting the CJ velocity:

\[
R \ll \frac{1}{\sqrt{G\,\rho_0}} \sqrt{\frac{2(\gamma^2-1)\,L}{\rho_0}}
\]

For a sufficiently energetic phase transition (\(L \gg G\,\rho_0^2\,R^2\)),
the detonation outpaces gravitational collapse and achieves homogenization.
At the extreme nuclear density \(\rho_0 \sim 10^{50}\) kg/m³ at which the
phase transition is expected to occur (cf. Popławski 2010 for an independent
estimate of this scale), this requires:

\[
L \gg G\,\rho_0^2\,R^2 \sim 10^{-11} \times 10^{100} \times R^2
= 10^{89}\,R^2 \quad [\text{J/m}^3 \cdot \text{m}^2]
\]

The fact that \(L\) scales with \(\rho_0\) (the energy released is proportional
to the density of matter undergoing transition) while the free-fall time scales
as \(\rho_0^{-1/2}\) means that *denser initial conditions favor
homogenization*. The black hole interior, being the densest environment in
nature, is thus optimally suited for this mechanism.

#### 3.3.7 Solving the Horizon Problem

In standard cosmology, the horizon problem arises because causally disconnected
regions of the CMB have the same temperature to within 1 part in \(10^5\).
Inflation solves this by exponentially expanding a causally connected patch.

The shockwave mechanism offers an alternative: the detonation wave, originating
from a single causally connected core, propagates outward and thermalizes the
post-transition material. Every point in the resulting universe was processed
by the same shock, establishing thermal contact. The residual temperature
variations (\(\sim 10^{-5}\)) arise from the density-dependent conversion
efficiency (Section 3.4), not from acausal initial conditions.

Unlike inflation, which requires a specific potential for the inflaton field and
fine-tuned initial conditions for slow roll, the shockwave mechanism follows
automatically from the energetics of the first-order phase transition. The
detonation velocity, shock structure, and homogenization timescale are all
determined by the Landau coefficients and the initial density — the same
parameters already constrained by \(\xi\).

### 3.4 Density-Dependent Conversion and Primordial Perturbations

The conversion efficiency is not perfectly uniform. The order parameter
equilibrium value \(\eta_{\text{eq}}\) depends on the local thermodynamic
conditions — specifically, the local energy density — at the time of the
phase transition.

#### 3.4.1 Perturbative Expansion

Let \(\bar{\rho}\) be the mean pre-transition density and
\(\delta_\rho(\mathbf{x}) = (\rho(\mathbf{x}) - \bar{\rho})/\bar{\rho}\) be
the fractional density perturbation inherited from the parent universe. The
local conversion efficiency can be expanded:

\[
\xi(\mathbf{x}) = \xi_0 + \xi_1\,\delta_\rho(\mathbf{x})
+ \frac{1}{2}\xi_2\,\delta_\rho^2(\mathbf{x}) + \mathcal{O}(\delta_\rho^3)
\]

where \(\xi_0 = 0.158\) is the mean conversion fraction and the coefficients
\(\xi_n\) encode the sensitivity of the phase transition to density variations.
From the Landau free energy, \(\xi_1 = d\xi/d\delta_\rho\) can be computed by
differentiating the equilibrium condition with respect to density (since
\(a(T)\), and thus \(T_c\), depend on density through the equation of state).

#### 3.4.2 Seeding Primordial Perturbations

The baryon density perturbation immediately after conversion is:

\[
\delta_b(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{\xi(\mathbf{x}) - \xi_0}{\xi_0}
= \frac{\xi_1}{\xi_0}\,\delta_\rho(\mathbf{x})
+ \frac{\xi_2}{2\xi_0}\,\delta_\rho^2(\mathbf{x}) + \cdots
\]

The linear term transfers the parent-universe density field into a baryon
perturbation with an amplitude amplification (or suppression) factor of
\(\xi_1/\xi_0\). The quadratic term generates non-Gaussianity even if the
parent-universe density field was Gaussian:

\[
\delta_b \supset \frac{\xi_2}{2\xi_0}\,\delta_\rho^2
\quad \Longrightarrow \quad
f_{\text{NL}}^{\text{local}} = \frac{5}{6}\frac{\xi_2}{\xi_1^2/\xi_0}
\]

#### 3.4.3 Primordial Power Spectrum

The primordial power spectrum of baryon perturbations is:

\[
P_b^{(\text{prim})}(k) = \left(\frac{\xi_1}{\xi_0}\right)^2 P_\rho^{(\text{parent})}(k)
\]

where \(P_\rho^{(\text{parent})}(k)\) is the power spectrum of density
perturbations in the black hole interior at the moment of transition. This
spectrum is determined by the pre-bang accretion history and the black hole's
internal density profile, *not* by quantum fluctuations of an inflaton.

**Predicted spectral shape.** If the black hole interior has a power-law
density profile \(\rho(r) \propto r^{-\alpha_{\text{BH}}}\) (as expected for
a virialized or quasi-static gravitational accumulation), then the
three-dimensional power spectrum of the density perturbation field scales as
\(P(k) \propto k^{n_s}\) with:

\[
n_s = 3 - \alpha_{\text{BH}}
\]

This follows from the Fourier transform of a radial power-law field in three
dimensions: the perturbation variance on scale \(k \sim 1/r\) inherits the
exponent of the real-space profile, offset by the spatial dimension.

The observed value \(n_s = 0.965 \pm 0.004\) (Planck Collaboration 2020a)
would imply \(\alpha_{\text{BH}} \approx 2.035\), close to an isothermal
density profile (\(\alpha = 2\)). This is a specific, testable prediction:
the spectral index is linked to the gravitational structure of the progenitor
black hole.

**Distinction from inflation.** Single-field slow-roll inflation predicts
\(n_s = 1 - 2\epsilon - \eta_V\) where \(\epsilon\) and \(\eta_V\) are the
slow-roll parameters — dimensionless ratios of derivatives of the inflaton
potential. The RODM prediction ties \(n_s\) to a *gravitational* quantity
(\(\alpha_{\text{BH}}\)) rather than a field-theory quantity. At the level of
a single number (\(n_s\)), the two predictions can be made to agree. The
distinguishing signatures are:

1. **Running of the spectral index.** Inflation predicts
   \(dn_s/d\ln k = -2\xi_V^2 - (n_s - 1)^2/(4 - n_s)\), which is
   \(\sim -10^{-3}\). The RODM prediction depends on the curvature of
   the density profile, \(d\alpha_{\text{BH}}/d\ln r\), which need not be
   small.

2. **Scale-dependent non-Gaussianity.** The quadratic term in
   \(\xi(\rho)\) produces scale-dependent \(f_{\text{NL}}\) tied to the
   shape of \(P_\rho^{(\text{parent})}(k)\), distinct from the nearly
   constant \(f_{\text{NL}}^{\text{local}}\) predicted by multi-field
   inflation models.

### 3.5 Cosmological Perturbation Theory for the Remnant Sector

We work in conformal Newtonian gauge with the perturbed
Friedmann--Robertson--Walker metric:

\[
ds^2 = a^2(\tau)\left[-(1 + 2\Psi)\,d\tau^2 + (1 - 2\Phi)\,\delta_{ij}\,dx^i\,dx^j\right]
\]

where \(\tau\) is conformal time, \(a(\tau)\) is the scale factor, and
\(\Phi\), \(\Psi\) are the Bardeen potentials (equal in the absence of
anisotropic stress: \(\Phi = \Psi\)).

#### 3.5.1 Two-Component Fluid

The universe after the conversion event contains two matter components:
baryons (fraction \(\xi\)) and remnant matter (fraction \(1-\xi\)). In the
fluid approximation, the perturbation equations for the remnant component are
(Ma & Bertschinger 1995):

**Continuity equation:**

\[
\dot{\delta}_R + \theta_R + 3\dot{\Phi} = 0
\]

**Euler equation:**

\[
\dot{\theta}_R + \mathcal{H}\theta_R - k^2\Psi = 0
\]

where \(\delta_R = \delta\rho_R/\bar{\rho}_R\) is the remnant density contrast,
\(\theta_R = ik_j v_R^j\) is the velocity divergence, \(\mathcal{H} = aH\)
is the conformal Hubble parameter, overdots denote conformal-time derivatives,
and \(k\) is the comoving wavenumber.

These equations are *identical* in form to standard CDM perturbation equations.
The crucial difference lies in the initial conditions.

#### 3.5.2 Initial Conditions: RODM vs. Standard CDM

**Standard \(\Lambda\)CDM:** Perturbations are seeded by quantum fluctuations
during inflation, producing adiabatic initial conditions with a nearly
scale-invariant spectrum \(P(k) \propto k^{n_s}\), \(n_s \approx 0.965\).
Baryons and CDM share the same primordial curvature perturbation \(\zeta\):

\[
\delta_b^{(\text{init})} = \delta_{\text{CDM}}^{(\text{init})} = -\frac{2}{3}\zeta
\]

**RODM:** The remnant matter carries its own perturbation spectrum inherited
from the parent universe:

\[
\delta_R^{(\text{init})}(k) \sim P_\rho^{(\text{parent})}(k)
\]

while the baryon perturbations are related to the remnant perturbations through
the conversion mechanism (Section 3.4):

\[
\delta_b^{(\text{init})}(k) = \frac{\xi_1}{\xi_0}\,\delta_R^{(\text{init})}(k)
\]

This produces *isocurvature-like* initial conditions: the ratio of baryon to
remnant perturbations is fixed by the microphysics of the phase transition,
not by a shared inflationary origin. However, unlike pure isocurvature modes
(which are tightly constrained by CMB data), the RODM initial conditions are
*correlated* — both components trace the same parent density field, just with
different amplitudes.

#### 3.5.3 Gravitational Potentials

The Poisson equation in Fourier space:

\[
k^2 \Phi = -4\pi G a^2 \left[
\xi\,\bar{\rho}_b\,\delta_b + (1-\xi)\,\bar{\rho}_R\,\delta_R
\right]
\]

Since remnant matter dominates (\(1-\xi \approx 0.84\)), the gravitational
potential is primarily sourced by \(\delta_R\), and the evolution of
\(\delta_b\) is largely driven by falling into remnant-matter potential wells.
This is phenomenologically identical to standard CDM at late times, which is
why RODM reproduces the successes of \(\Lambda\)CDM for large-scale structure
and CMB acoustic peaks.

### 3.6 Transfer Function, Power Spectrum, and Non-Gaussianity

#### 3.6.1 Transfer Function

The transfer function \(T(k)\) relates the primordial power spectrum to the
late-time matter power spectrum:

\[
P(k, z) = T^2(k)\,D^2(z)\,P^{(\text{prim})}(k)
\]

where \(D(z)\) is the linear growth factor. For standard CDM, the transfer
function is well approximated by the Eisenstein & Hu (1998) fitting formulae,
which encode the effects of radiation-matter equality, baryon acoustic
oscillations, and Silk damping.

For RODM, the remnant component has the same perturbation equations as CDM
(Section 3.5.1), so the *shape* of the transfer function at scales below the
horizon at matter-radiation equality is identical. The difference appears in:

1. **The primordial spectrum.** \(P^{(\text{prim})}(k)\) may deviate from a
   simple power law if the parent-universe density profile has structure
   (Section 3.4.3).

2. **Large-scale initial conditions.** Scales that were super-horizon at the
   time of the transition may carry imprints of the parent-universe geometry,
   producing features in \(P(k)\) at \(k \lesssim 10^{-3}\) Mpc\(^{-1}\).

#### 3.6.2 Total Matter Power Spectrum

The total matter power spectrum receives contributions from both components:

\[
P_{\text{tot}}(k) = \xi^2\, P_b(k) + (1-\xi)^2\, P_R(k)
+ 2\xi(1-\xi)\, P_{bR}(k)
\]

where \(P_{bR}(k)\) is the baryon-remnant cross-spectrum. Because both
components trace the same underlying parent density field (with the linear
relation \(\delta_b = (\xi_1/\xi_0)\,\delta_R\)), the cross-spectrum is:

\[
P_{bR}(k) = \frac{\xi_1}{\xi_0}\, P_R(k)
\]

and the total simplifies to:

\[
P_{\text{tot}}(k) = \left[(1-\xi) + \xi\frac{\xi_1}{\xi_0}\right]^2 P_R(k)
\]

If \(\xi_1/\xi_0 \approx 1\) (weak density dependence of conversion),
\(P_{\text{tot}} \approx P_R\), recovering standard CDM phenomenology. Larger
values of \(\xi_1/\xi_0\) produce observable deviations.

#### 3.6.3 Non-Gaussianity (Two-Source Model)

The RODM model predicts two distinct, additive sources of local-type
non-Gaussianity, yielding a parameter-free ratio that distinguishes it
from single-field inflation.

Define \(\gamma \equiv \xi_1/\xi_0 = \alpha_{\text{BH}} = 3 - n_s\), where
\(n_s\) is the measured scalar spectral index. With Planck 2018
(\(n_s = 0.9649\)), \(\gamma = 2.0351\).

**Source 1 — Baryon conversion nonlinearity.** The density-dependent
conversion \(\xi(\rho)\) (Section 3.4.2) has a quadratic Taylor expansion:

\[
\delta_b = \frac{\xi_1}{\xi_0}\,\delta_R
+ \frac{\xi_2}{2\xi_0}\,\delta_R^2, \qquad
\xi_2 = \xi_0\,\gamma(\gamma - 1)
\]

The standard local non-Gaussianity parameter is
\(f_{\text{NL}} = (5/6) \times [\text{quadratic coefficient}] /
[\text{linear coefficient}]^2\). Substituting:

\[
f_{\text{NL}}^{(\text{CMB})} = \frac{5}{12}\,\frac{\gamma - 1}{\gamma}
= \frac{5}{12}\,\frac{1.0351}{2.0351} = 0.212
\]

This is the non-Gaussianity visible in the CMB, which traces baryonic
perturbations processed through the conversion and subsequent recombination.

**Source 2 — Gravitational induction of remnant non-Gaussianity.** The
remnant sector inherits a Gaussian density field from the parent universe.
However, the Poisson equation couples both sectors:

\[
k^2 \Phi = -4\pi G a^2 \bigl[\xi\,\rho_b\,\delta_b
+ (1 - \xi)\,\rho_R\,\delta_R\bigr]
\]

The baryon quadratic term \(\propto \delta_R^2\) sources a quadratic
correction to the gravitational potential \(\Phi\), which feeds back into
the remnant's evolution, *inducing* non-Gaussianity:

\[
f_{\text{NL}}^{(\text{remnant})} = \frac{5}{12}\,\frac{1}{\gamma}
= \frac{5}{12}\,\frac{1}{2.0351} = 0.205
\]

**Total lensing signal.** Weak lensing is sensitive to total matter
(baryons + remnant), so both sources add:

\[
f_{\text{NL}}^{(\text{lens})}
= f_{\text{NL}}^{(\text{CMB})} + f_{\text{NL}}^{(\text{remnant})}
= \frac{5}{12}\,\frac{\gamma - 1}{\gamma} + \frac{5}{12}\,\frac{1}{\gamma}
= \frac{5}{12} = 0.417
\]

Note: the sum is exactly \(5/12\) independent of \(\gamma\) — this is a
cross-check on the algebra.

**The signature prediction** is the ratio:

\[
\boxed{\frac{f_{\text{NL}}^{(\text{lens})}}{f_{\text{NL}}^{(\text{CMB})}}
= \frac{\gamma}{\gamma - 1} = \frac{3 - n_s}{2 - n_s} = 1.97}
\]

This ratio is parameter-free: it depends only on the measured spectral tilt
\(n_s\) and the assumption \(\gamma = 3 - n_s\) (that conversion sensitivity
tracks the density profile slope). In standard \(\Lambda\)CDM, both channels
should yield the same \(f_{\text{NL}}\) (ratio = 1.0), since both trace the
same primordial perturbations. A statistically significant excess in lensing
non-Gaussianity over CMB non-Gaussianity is a smoking-gun signature of RODM.

Current Planck constraints give \(f_{\text{NL}}^{\text{local}} = -0.9 \pm 5.1\)
from CMB (Planck Collaboration 2020b). Both RODM predictions (0.212 and 0.417)
lie well within this bound. Euclid and Rubin/LSST will measure
\(f_{\text{NL}}\) from weak lensing with comparable or better precision within
the decade — sufficient to resolve the 1.97× ratio at >3σ if the signal exists.

#### 3.6.4 Halo Density Profiles

In standard \(\Lambda\)CDM, dark matter halos form through hierarchical
gravitational collapse, producing the universal Navarro--Frenk--White (NFW)
density profile (Navarro, Frenk & White 1996):

\[
\rho_{\text{NFW}}(r) = \frac{\rho_s}{(r/r_s)(1 + r/r_s)^2}
\]

with an inner slope \(d\ln\rho/d\ln r = -1\) as \(r \to 0\).

In RODM, remnant matter clumps are *pre-formed* — they are density
concentrations inherited from the parent universe, not assembled through
hierarchical merging. The inner profile is instead:

\[
\rho_{\text{RODM}}(r) = \frac{\rho_s}{(r/r_s)^\alpha\,(1 + r/r_s)^{3-\alpha}}
\]

The inner slope \(\alpha\) depends on whether remnant clumps have residual
kinematic support:

- If remnant matter is purely cold and collisionless (no residual velocities
  from the parent universe), clumps are more concentrated:
  \(\alpha > 1\) (steeper cusps).

- If remnant clumps retain some velocity dispersion from their parent-universe
  origin, pressure support softens the inner profile:
  \(\alpha < 1\) (cores).

Either case predicts \(\alpha \neq 1\) at a level distinguishable from NFW.
High-resolution strong-lensing observations and stellar-kinematic measurements
in dwarf galaxies can constrain \(\alpha\) to \(\pm 0.1\)
(e.g., Adams et al. 2014).

#### 3.6.5 Subhalo Mass Function

The subhalo mass function — the number of dark matter substructures as a
function of mass within a host halo — is a key discriminant. In
\(\Lambda\)CDM, the Aquarius simulation predicts
\(dN/dM \propto M^{-1.9}\) (Springel et al. 2008).

RODM predicts a *steeper* mass function because remnant clumps:

1. Are pre-formed and more resistant to tidal disruption (no internal energy
   dissipation channels).
2. Lack the hierarchical assembly that smooths out the CDM mass function at
   low masses.
3. May carry sub-clump structure inherited from the parent universe,
   preserving small-scale power.

The predicted slope:

\[
\frac{dN}{dM} \propto M^{-\beta}, \quad \beta > 1.9 \quad (\text{RODM})
\]

A detection of \(\beta - 1.9 > 0\) at \(> 2\sigma\) significance through
substructure lensing or gaps in stellar streams would support RODM.

## 6. Discussion

### 6.1 Relationship to Prior Frameworks

RODM draws on prior work by Smolin and Popławski as motivation, but departs
from both in its central mechanism. From Smolin's cosmological natural
selection, it takes the parent-child universe topology — the idea that black
holes produce new cosmic domains. From Popławski's torsion cosmology, it
takes only the observation that extreme densities inside black hole interiors
are physically significant; the torsion-bounce mechanism itself is not adopted.

RODM's own mechanism is a first-order phase transition: the scale field
\(\sigma(x)\) rises with local density until bond-failure thresholds are
crossed sequentially, igniting a Chapman--Jouguet detonation. Homogeneity,
flatness, and the horizon solution all follow from the detonation shockwave
geometry — not from torsion, spin-curvature coupling, or elastic rebound.
This distinction matters: the bounce is a conservative (elastic) process that
preserves the pre-existing matter content; RODM's detonation is a dissipative,
irreversible phase conversion that changes the matter content and
gauge-compatibility of the entire causal domain.

### 6.2 The Shockwave as Alternative to Inflation

The RODM shockwave mechanism addresses the horizon problem — why the universe
is homogeneous on scales larger than the causal horizon — through a physical
detonation rather than superluminal expansion. The key advantage is parsimony:
no new field (the inflaton) or fine-tuned potential is required. The detonation
follows automatically from the energetics of the same phase transition that
creates baryonic matter.

However, the mechanism does not address all problems that inflation solves.
In particular:

- **Monopole problem.** If the phase transition produces topological defects,
  the shockwave may or may not dilute them. This requires further analysis.
- **Tensor-to-scalar ratio.** Inflation predicts a specific relationship
  between gravitational wave amplitude and \(n_s\). RODM's prediction for
  the primordial gravitational wave background depends on the details of the
  detonation, which remain to be calculated.

### 6.3 Bond-Failure Layers: Physical Basis and Caveats

The SSBM model predicts five sequential bond-failure zones inside a black hole,
defined by the condition that the fourth root of the Kretschner curvature
invariant matches the characteristic length scale of each bond type:

\[
K^{1/4} = \frac{1}{\ell}, \quad \text{where } K = \frac{48\,G^2 M^2}{c^4\, r^6}
\]

Solving for \(r\) yields the layer radius formula:
\(r = (48\,G^2 M^2 / c^4)^{1/6} \cdot \ell^{2/3}\).

**Why \(K^{1/4} = 1/\ell\) and not classical tidal disruption.**
Classical tidal disruption occurs when the differential gravitational
acceleration across a bound structure exceeds the binding force per unit mass.
This gives a *different* condition — and for Sgr A\* nuclear bonds, tidal
disruption occurs at \(\sim 3\times\) the SSBM layer radius. The SSBM
condition is distinct: it posits that bonds fail not when they are pulled apart
by tidal stretching, but when the local curvature wavelength becomes
commensurate with the bond scale — i.e., when spacetime geometry itself is
structured at the same scale as the bond. This is a stronger, more fundamental
condition than tidal disruption and occurs deeper in the gravitational well.
The physical justification is that at \(K^{1/4} \sim 1/\ell\), the curvature
is no longer a perturbation on the scale of the bond but becomes the dominant
geometric feature, invalidating the flat-spacetime approximation on which
the bond's existence depends. A full derivation from the effective action
of the scale field \(\sigma(x)\) coupled to the Kretschner invariant is
required to place this condition on rigorous footing.

**Progressive failure zones, not sharp boundaries.**
Each bond type spans a range of binding energies. Nuclear binding energy
per nucleon varies from \(\sim 1.1\) MeV (deuterium) to \(\sim 8.8\) MeV
(iron/nickel), a factor of \(\sim 8\times\). In practice, the "nuclear
bond-failure layer" is a *zone* in which nuclei fail progressively: deuterium
first (at larger radius), then lithium, carbon, oxygen, and finally iron-group
nuclides at the inner edge. The same applies to molecular and atomic layers.
The five-layer picture is a useful idealization that captures the correct
ordering and energy hierarchy, but the actual structure is a continuous
gradient of progressive bond failure rather than five discrete shells.

**Temporal, not spatial, interpretation inside the horizon.**
Inside a Schwarzschild black hole (\(r < R_s\)), the radial coordinate \(r\)
becomes timelike and the time coordinate becomes spacelike. A "layer at
\(r = r_0\)" is therefore not a static spherical shell but a *moment in the
infalling matter's history* — the coordinate time at which the curvature
reaches the bond-failure threshold. The Kretschner scalar
\(K = 48\,G^2 M^2 / (c^4 r^6)\) is a coordinate-invariant quantity and
remains physically meaningful regardless of the signature of \(r\).
Infalling matter experiences the five failure thresholds as a temporal
sequence — Van der Waals bonds fail first (outermost \(r\), earliest time),
followed by molecular, atomic, nuclear, and finally QCD confinement
(smallest \(r\), latest time before the singularity). The concentric-shell
visualization used elsewhere in this paper should be understood as a
schematic of this temporal ordering rather than a static spatial arrangement.

### 6.4 Speculative Extension: Dark Energy as Residual Conversion Energy

While the core RODM model addresses dark matter, a natural question arises:
could the same phase transition also account for dark energy?

During the conversion event, parent-universe matter undergoes a first-order
phase transition from an incompatible state to locally compatible baryonic
matter. This transition releases latent heat (Section 3.2). However, the
conversion need not be energetically complete. If the binding energies and
vacuum structure of the parent universe differ from our own, the energy
liberated during conversion may not fully thermalize into our universe's
degrees of freedom.

Specifically: the baryonic binding energy of the parent-universe matter — the
energy that held its atoms, nuclei, and fields together under the parent
universe's physical laws — has no corresponding "receptacle" in our gauge
structure. When matter converts, this incompatible binding energy is released
but cannot be absorbed by any Standard Model field. It persists as a residual
energy density permeating the vacuum.

If this residual energy density has an equation of state \(w \approx -1\)
(i.e., it acts as a negative-pressure fluid), it would be observationally
indistinguishable from a cosmological constant. The observed dark energy
density \(\Omega_\Lambda \approx 0.685\) (Planck Collaboration 2020a) would
then be determined by the difference in vacuum energy between the parent and
daughter universes:

\[
\rho_\Lambda = \epsilon_{\text{binding}}^{(\text{parent})}
- \epsilon_{\text{binding}}^{(\text{local})}
\]

where \(\epsilon_{\text{binding}}^{(\text{parent})}\) is the vacuum energy
density of the parent-universe matter and
\(\epsilon_{\text{binding}}^{(\text{local})}\) is the vacuum energy density
of locally compatible matter.

**This extension is speculative** and is not part of the core falsifiable
RODM hypothesis. It is presented here as a direction for future
investigation. Key questions include: (a) whether the residual energy would
naturally produce \(w \approx -1\) rather than some other equation of state,
(b) whether the magnitude is consistent with the observed
\(\rho_\Lambda \sim 10^{-29}\) g/cm³ without fine-tuning, and (c) whether
the time dependence of dark energy (if any) could distinguish this mechanism
from a bare cosmological constant.

If this extension holds, the RODM framework would provide a unified
cosmogonic account of all three major components of the universe's energy
budget: baryonic matter (converted), dark matter (unconverted remnant), and
dark energy (residual incompatible binding energy).

### 6.5 Entropy, Heat Death, and the Nova Cycle

A natural question raised by the black-hole Nova cycle is whether RODM
provides a mechanism for indefinite energy recycling — and whether this
conflicts with the second law of thermodynamics.

**The second law is preserved within each universe.** Entropy increases
monotonically inside any given causal domain. Stars burn, structure forms and
dissipates, black holes accumulate entropy (Bekenstein-Hawking surface
entropy grows as \(S \propto M^2\)), and the parent universe trends toward
a maximum-entropy terminal state. SSBM introduces no violation of the second
law at any step in this progression.

**The Nova event reframes heat death rather than preventing it.** When a
black hole reaches radial criticality and the conversion detonation fires,
the shockwave homogenizes the accreted matter — a strongly entropy-increasing
process in the parent universe's thermodynamic frame. The ejected 15.82%
leaves the parent's causal domain entirely, entering a new universe starting
at extremely low entropy: hot, dense, and homogenized by the same shockwave
that increased entropy in the parent. The thermodynamic accounting is
internally consistent: the entropy increase in the parent directly *funds*
the low-entropy initial condition of the child. There is no free lunch.

**What RODM does challenge is the finality of heat death.** The standard
heat-death picture treats the universe as a closed system with a single
terminal state. RODM makes the universe *causally leaky*: its most entropic
objects — gravitationally collapsed black holes, the endpoints of stellar
evolution — are simultaneously the exits through which energy propagates into
new low-entropy domains. Heat death in any given universe is not a terminus
but a branching point.

**The meta-system does not have a global entropy.** The full tree of nested
universes is not a thermodynamic system with a single entropy function. Each
branch has its own entropy clock, its own vacuum structure, and its own
second law. There is no frame in which all branches can be aggregated into a
single thermodynamic state. The concept of universal heat death therefore
applies locally (within each universe) but has no well-defined meaning
globally. In this sense, RODM is consistent with — but structurally more
optimistic than — the Boltzmann-Kelvin heat death picture.

**A constraint this places on the cycle.** For the branching to be
indefinitely sustained, each child universe must itself produce black holes
that reach criticality. This requires the child's physical constants (and
in particular ξ) to fall within a range compatible with stellar evolution and
gravitational collapse. Whether cosmological natural selection drives ξ toward
such values — as Smolin proposed for dimensionless constants generally — is an
open question that RODM inherits from its parent framework.

**Parent black hole mass and child universe quality.** Not all Nova events are
equal. The homogenization assumption holds cleanly for stellar-mass and
intermediate-mass black holes, whose post-Nova accretion is limited to their
immediate environment. Supermassive black holes at galactic centers, however,
have the entire host galaxy as a feeding reservoir. Post-Nova accretion from
this extended structure continues at high rates long after the conversion event,
delivering late-arriving mass asymmetrically along the accretion disk geometry.
The result is a child universe with lumpier initial conditions: higher CMB
anisotropy, stronger inherited angular momentum from the parent's spin, and
less uniform large-scale structure. Our own CMB uniformity (δT/T ~ 10⁻⁵)
suggests a relatively quiet parent environment — consistent with a stellar or
intermediate-mass black hole in a low-accretion state at the time of conversion,
rather than an active galactic nucleus. This introduces a natural habitability
filter: supermassive black holes in AGN phases may produce universes too
inhomogeneous for stable long-lived stellar chemistry, while quieter, smaller
black holes produce the clean initial conditions necessary for structure
formation and life. Cosmological natural selection may therefore select not just
for ξ, but for parent black hole mass and accretion environment.

### 6.6 What RODM Does Not Explain

- **Baryon asymmetry details.** While RODM provides the *amount* of baryonic
  matter (\(\xi\)), it does not explain why the conversion produces a
  matter-antimatter asymmetry. This may be a property inherited from the
  parent universe or may require additional mechanisms during the phase
  transition.
- **Parent universe properties.** The model treats the parent universe as a
  boundary condition. It does not explain why the parent universe exists, what
  its physical laws are, or why it produces black holes. This is an irreducible
  assumption.
- **Dark energy (core model).** The core RODM model is agnostic about dark
  energy. The speculative extension in Section 6.4 offers a possible
  mechanism but requires substantial additional development to be testable.

### 6.7 Limitations

1. **The Landau coefficients are underdetermined.** The constraint \(\xi = 0.158\)
   provides one relationship among three free parameters (\(a_0\), \(b\), \(c\)).
   Additional observables (e.g., the latent heat, inferred from a gravitational
   wave background) are needed to fully specify the phase transition.

2. **The parent-universe density profile is unknown.** The primordial power
   spectrum prediction (Section 3.4.3) depends on \(\alpha_{\text{BH}}\), which
   is not predicted by the model but must be measured.

3. **Numerical simulations are needed.** The fluid-approximation perturbation
   theory presented here is a first step. A complete treatment requires
   modifying a Boltzmann solver (e.g., CLASS or CAMB) to incorporate
   RODM initial conditions and running N-body simulations with pre-formed
   remnant clumps.

### 6.8 Future Directions

- Modify the CLASS Boltzmann solver to accept RODM initial conditions and
  compute CMB and matter power spectra.
- Run N-body simulations with pre-formed remnant clumps to predict halo
  profiles and subhalo mass functions.
- Calculate the gravitational wave spectrum from the first-order phase
  transition detonation for comparison with LISA sensitivity curves.
- Investigate whether the phase transition naturally produces a
  matter-antimatter asymmetry through CP-violating processes during
  conversion.
- Develop the dark energy extension (Section 6.4) into a quantitative model:
  derive the equation of state of residual incompatible binding energy and
  compare with observational constraints on \(w\).

## 7. Conclusion

We have presented the Remnant-Origin Dark Matter (RODM) hypothesis: a
cosmogonic model in which dark matter is unconverted matter inherited from a
parent universe during the first-order phase transition that initiated our
Big Bang. The model is grounded in the effective action for a gravitationally
coupled scalar field, constrained by Landau--Ginzburg thermodynamics to
reproduce the observed baryon-to-dark-matter ratio, and supplemented by a
Chapman--Jouguet detonation mechanism that solves the horizon problem through
physical homogenization rather than inflationary expansion.

The model makes six falsifiable predictions, including a lensing
non-Gaussianity excess over CMB non-Gaussianity (the strongest discriminant),
a null signal in all direct dark matter detection experiments (testable to the
neutrino floor by DARWIN/XLZD in the 2030s), a steeper-than-CDM subhalo mass
function, anomalous inner halo density profiles, and a specific link between
primordial spectral running and the progenitor black hole's density structure.

These predictions are testable with instruments that are either currently
operating (LZ, XENONnT, Euclid) or under construction (Rubin/LSST, Roman,
DARWIN/XLZD, CMB-S4). The coming decade will determine whether RODM is a
viable alternative to particle dark matter or whether direct detection of a
dark matter particle renders it moot.
