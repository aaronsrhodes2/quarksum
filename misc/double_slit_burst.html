▸ The Full Loop — how this simulation works, in plain English ▶

The complete chain from physical constants to the pixel you see on screen. ■ PHYSICS — affects where particles land.   ■ DISPLAY — affects only how it looks.   ■ CASCADE — where the self-consistency of constants is required.

Step 1 — The constants arrive from the cascade  CASCADE

Before a single particle is fired, three numbers are loaded from local_library/constants.py:

  • ℏ = 1.054571817 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s — the fundamental quantum of action. Every wave in quantum mechanics has its scale set by this number.
  • m_e = 0.51099895 MeV/c² — the electron mass. Stored in energy units, converted to kilograms via m_e(kg) = m_e(MeV) × e / c².
  • e = 1.602176634 × 10⁻¹⁹ C — the electron charge. Used to convert "1 electron-volt" into joules, and MeV into kilograms.

◈ These three must be mutually consistent — drawn from the same anchored source with no rounding at intermediate steps. If you use a precise ℏ but a rounded e, the computed wavelength is slightly wrong and the predicted fringe positions disagree with where particles actually land. The cascade guarantees they all trace back to the same chain of derivations. No magic numbers.

Step 2 — The electron's wavelength is computed  PHYSICS

A 1 eV electron has momentum p = √(2 m_e E). Its de Broglie wavelength is λ = h / p where h = 2πℏ.
Result: λ = 1.2264 nm. This is the "size" of the electron's wave — smaller than an atom, larger than a proton. It determines how far apart the bright stripes are.
Fringe spacing: Δy = λL/d = 1.2264 mm. The blue tick marks on each screen show exactly where those peaks should appear.

Step 3 — The electron goes through both slits at once  PHYSICS

The electron is not a tiny ball that picks one slit. Before it is measured, it is a wave — spread out, passing through both gaps simultaneously, the same way a water ripple spreads through two gaps in a barrier at once. Each slit becomes a new source of expanding ripples. At any point y on the screen, those two ripples have travelled slightly different distances: r₁ from the top slit, r₂ from the bottom slit.

Step 4 — The path difference is computed with care  PHYSICS  CASCADE

Are the two ripples in step (peaks meeting peaks → bright) or out of step (peak meeting trough → dark)? That depends on k · Δr, where k = 2π/λ and Δr = r₁ − r₂.

Here is the problem: at 10 cm distance and λ = 1.2 nm, k · L ≈ 500,000,000 radians. A computer stores about 15 significant figures. Computing k·r₁ and k·r₂ separately and subtracting loses almost all precision — like subtracting 500,000,000.123 from 500,000,000.456 and trusting the 0.333 that remains.

The fix: compute Δr = −2yd / (r₁+r₂) directly using an algebraic identity. This gives a number around 1 nanometre with full precision preserved. ◈ CASCADE: λ must be self-consistent with k = 2π/λ. Any mismatch from using constants from different sources shifts all fringe positions.

Step 5 — The probability at every screen position is computed  PHYSICS

P(y) = |A₁|² + |A₂|²  +  2·√(1−D²)·|A₁|·|A₂|·cos(k·Δr)  ×  sinc²(πa sinθ / λ)

Three parts:

  • |A₁|² + |A₂|² — the background: what you'd get if both slits illuminated the screen independently with no wave interaction.
  • 2·√(1−D²)·|A₁|·|A₂|·cos(k·Δr) — the interference term. This oscillates between positive (bright fringe) and negative (dark fringe) as y moves across the screen. D is the observer: D=0 leaves this term fully intact; D=1 kills it entirely (√(1−1)=0). Partial D partially suppresses it.
  • sinc²(πa sinθ/λ) — the single-slit envelope. Each slit has a finite width a=20 nm. This acts as a gentle hill that multiplies the whole pattern, fading fringes far from centre. The orange dashed ticks show where it reaches zero.

Step 6 — The probability is turned into a sampling map  PHYSICS

P(y) is evaluated at 3,000 points and integrated (trapezoid rule) to give a cumulative probability: "what fraction of all particles land at or below position y?" This is the CDF — a smooth curve from 0 to 1. Steep where fringes are bright. Nearly flat where fringes are dark.

Step 7 — Each particle is fired: Born-rule sampling  PHYSICS

  1. Draw a random number r between 0 and 1.
  2. Ask the CDF map: "what y has cumulative probability exactly r?"
  3. Find it by binary search and linear interpolation.
  4. That y is the hit position — a physically valid measurement outcome.

This is the Born rule: each particle lands at a random position, but with probability proportional to |ψ(y)|² = P(y). No particle knows where the others landed. No hidden plan. The pattern emerges purely from the statistics of many independent draws.

The physics ends here. The y-position from Step 7 is the only thing the simulation knows that is physically real. Everything that follows is about how to show it to you.

Step 8 — The hit goes in a bin  DISPLAY

The screen is divided into 300 equal vertical bins. The hit increments a counter for whichever bin y falls into. The precise y within the bin is discarded.

Step 9 — Brightness is computed from the count and time  DISPLAY

brightness = (bin_count / max_count) × exp(−t / τ)
All particles were fired at t=0, so all bins start at full brightness and fade at the same rate. The pattern never changes — only the overall brightness drops. The decay formula is correct physics; the value τ=4 s is chosen so your eyes can follow it. Real phosphor screens decay in under a tenth of a second.

Step 10 — Brightness becomes colour and is drawn  DISPLAY

Brightness (0–1) maps through a colour ramp: black → dark green → bright green → yellow-white. This mimics a classic phosphorescent detector screen. The colour carries no physical information — it is chosen because it is readable and because the contrast makes fringe structure easy to see. A plain table of bin counts would contain identical physics. A subtle edge-fade (pixels near the left and right canvas borders are slightly dimmer) makes the screen look like a physical tube. Purely cosmetic.

Why the cascade is load-bearing, not optional

The constants ℏ, m_e, and e must be mutually consistent for two concrete reasons:

  • Fringe position accuracy: Fringes are spaced 1.2264 mm apart. Matching that to four decimal places requires λ accurate to the same level, which requires ℏ and m_e consistent to full computer precision. A 0.01% error in m_e shifts the fringes by ~12 µm — detectable in a careful experiment.
  • Phase precision: k·Δr must be accurate to better than one part in 10⁸ to correctly place each fringe. Any inconsistency in constants corrupts the phase, and fringes appear in the wrong positions. The simulation would look fine but predict incorrectly — which is the most dangerous kind of wrong.

The cascade in local_library exists to prevent this: every constant is derived from the same anchored chain. If you ran the Python prediction file (test_double_slit_predictions.py) against this HTML, fringe positions would match to four significant figures — because both use the same cascade.

▸ The Renderer & The Edge — what is physics, what is display ▶

✓ PHYSICS — what this simulation does honestly

  • Intensity formula: P(y) = (|A₁|²+|A₂|²) + 2√(1−D²)·|A₁||A₂|·cos(kΔr) · sinc²(πa sinθ/λ)
    This is exact Huygens-Fresnel with partial decoherence (Englert 1996) and a single-slit diffraction envelope (Fraunhofer). No approximations beyond far-field geometry.
  • Numerical stability: Path difference computed as Δr = −2yd / (r₁+r₂) (difference-of-squares identity). This avoids catastrophic cancellation when computing cos(k·r₁) − cos(k·r₂) with k·L ≈ 5×10⁸ rad. Float64 only has ~15 significant digits; without this trick, the fringe phase would be garbage.
  • Born-rule sampling: Inverse-CDF method. A uniform random number is mapped through the cumulative probability distribution of P(y) to produce a hit position. This is mathematically identical to drawing from |ψ(y)|².
  • Fringe visibility measurement: Local contrast at predicted peak/valley positions (y=0 vs y=Δy/2), not global max/min. Global would confuse the diffraction envelope shape with fringe contrast.
  • All constants from local_library/constants.py cascade: ℏ=1.054571817×10⁻³⁴ J·s, c=2.99792458×10⁸ m/s, e=1.602176634×10⁻¹⁹ C, m_e=0.51099895 MeV/c². No magic numbers.
  • Englert duality D²+V²≤1 is satisfied by construction: the coherence factor √(1−D²) guarantees it analytically.

⚠ NOT_PHYSICS — display choices that are art, not science

  • Phosphor colormap: The green glow is aesthetic. Real ZnS:Ag emits at ~450 nm (blue-green). The colour ramp — black → deep green → bright green → yellow-white — was chosen because it looks like a classic oscilloscope screen, not because it matches any emission spectrum. The colour tells you nothing about the physics.
  • Horizontal intensity falloff: Each screen bin fades toward the edges using fade = 1 − (distX)² × 0.30. This makes the screen look like a physical phosphor tube with limb darkening. It is purely cosmetic. The actual hit distribution is one-dimensional (y only). There is no x-axis physics.
  • Default τ = 4 seconds: Real ZnS:Ag phosphor: τ ≈ 25–50 ms. We use 4 seconds so you can see the glow decay with human eyes. The formula I(t)=I₀·exp(−t/τ) is correct physics; the value of τ is a display choice.
  • Bin discretization: The screen is divided into 300 vertical bins. Real detectors have finite pixel size too, but our bin count is chosen for visual resolution, not to model any specific detector.

◈ The Edge — where simulation ends and display begins

The simulation ends the moment sampleHit() returns a y-position. That number is real physics: a Born-rule draw from |ψ(y)|². Everything after that is rendering. The bin it falls into, the colour it gets painted, the glow that decays — none of that affects the physics. You could display the hit positions as a spreadsheet of numbers and the physics would be identical. The green glow is a window into the data, not the data itself.

The cyan theory curve shown in the other visualisations (not here) is |ψ(y)|² normalised for display. It is the ground truth the particles are converging toward. The phosphor image is the statistical sample. With enough particles, they match. With few, they don't — and that gap is quantum mechanics, not a rendering bug.
