Known limitation: Fixed timestep + high eccentricity.
At perihelion, Halley moves ~70 km/s. With dt=1 day, each step covers 6×10⁶ km — grossly undersampling the gravitational potential well. The symplectic integrator is stable (no NaN, no escape), but the trajectory diverges chaotically from the true orbit.

What would fix it: Adaptive timestep (dt ∝ r/v), higher-order integrator (Forest-Ruth 4th order), or regularization (Kustaanheimo-Stiefel transformation for close encounters).

Why it's not a failure: The integrator conserves energy to 10⁻⁶ and angular momentum to 10⁻¹⁵. The error is in phase space position, not in conservation laws. For circular/low-e orbits (Jovian system), the engine is excellent. For chaotic orbits, it's a documented limitation with a clear upgrade path.

6. Verdict

The n-body engine is solid for its design space.

For circular and low-eccentricity orbits (planets, Galilean moons, binary stars): energy conservation ~10⁻⁶, angular momentum ~10⁻¹⁵, Laplace resonance maintained at 178° over 26 years. The vectorized engine does 110K steps/sec — fast enough for real-time simulation of small systems.

For chaotic/high-eccentricity orbits (comets, asteroids, Earth-crossers): the fixed-timestep limitation dominates. Phase errors grow exponentially. The engine is stable (no crashes, no energy blow-up) but positionally inaccurate. This is the documented upgrade path.

σ-field integration works. The OOP engine correctly scales masses by e^σ, recovers Newtonian gravity at σ=0, and increases gravitational attraction for σ>0. The QCD-gravity coupling channel is live and testable in orbital dynamics.

Next upgrade priorities:
1. Adaptive timestep (for high-e orbits) — biggest accuracy win
2. J₂ oblateness (for giant planets) — fixes outer moon phase drift
3. Forest-Ruth 4th-order integrator — better energy conservation per step
